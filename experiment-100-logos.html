The brief: a hypothetical fintech brand targeting first-time investors in India. Young audience, first real income, equal parts anxiety and aspiration about money.

I generated 100 logo concepts across a single afternoon using a combination of Midjourney and DALL·E. To prevent the volume from becoming noise, each prompt was constrained to a single brand attribute: trust, momentum, approachability, precision, or growth. Twenty concepts per attribute.

The real value wasn't in any individual output — it was in seeing the shape of the landscape. Across 100 concepts, patterns emerged that I hadn't anticipated: a visual tension between "precision" and "approachability" that the brief had treated as compatible, and a cluster of momentum-led marks that kept drifting toward aggression rather than optimism.

Three territories emerged that weren't part of the original brief: softness as financial competence (warmth as trust signal, not friendliness), geometric abstraction with organic interruptions, and typography-only marks that implied motion through rhythm rather than imagery.

One of the three new territories became the direction I'd actually pursue. The other two became equally valuable: they were the "what we decided against" story, which clients always need.

The ability to show 97 directions you didn't take — and articulate why — is more persuasive than three directions you present neutrally. Rejection is a form of creative argument.

Bring a client into this process in real time. Watching a client react to 100 concepts — the instinctive attraction, the explained rejection, the things they can't articulate — is more valuable than any brief. You learn what they actually want rather than what they said they wanted.

Also: 100 is the right number. Enough to see the landscape, not so many that curation becomes impossible. Below 50, the territory feels incomplete. Above 150, you're generating noise.
