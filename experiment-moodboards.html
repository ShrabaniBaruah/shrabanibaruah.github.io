Traditional moodboards borrow from the world's existing visual language. They're derivative by design. AI offers something different: aesthetics that don't quite exist yet.

Every reference image carries baggage. Show a client a Nike campaign and they've already decided the ceiling. Show them an editorial from Vogue and half the room is thinking about licensing. The moment you point at something that exists, you've started negotiating with it.

A moodboard is supposed to evoke direction, not prescribe it. In practice, it usually prescribes it — because the references are too specific, too loaded, too owned.

The AI boards scored consistently lower on "clarity of direction" but higher on "unexpected energy." They also eliminated the reference-anchoring problem entirely. Teams couldn't point at anything and say "let's just make it look like that" — because there was no "that" to point at.

The most interesting outcome: AI boards surfaced disagreement earlier. Because the images weren't recognisable, teams had to articulate their reactions in words rather than pointing. That conversation — the one that usually happens six weeks later after the first round of work — happened in the room during alignment.

AI boards are harder to share across teams without context. A traditional moodboard communicates something even when you're not in the room to explain it. An AI board often looks like a collection of almost-right images that nobody quite trusts.

There's also a craft gap. The best traditional moodboards are themselves a form of curation and composition — the way the images sit together tells a story. AI boards don't yet have that layered authorship.

Use AI boards in early ideation as provocation, not as direction. The moment you show a client a reference image, you've set a ceiling. AI boards let you describe the ceiling without showing one.

Run them in combination: AI boards for the first conversation, traditional references to confirm territory once it's agreed. The AI clears the space; the references confirm you've arrived somewhere real.
