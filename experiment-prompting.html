AI — Image Generation

Prompting as
Art Direction

2025 Midjourney, DALL·E, Adobe Firefly Personal Experiment · 2 Weeks

A good brief doesn't describe what something looks like — it describes what it feels like. The same is true for a prompt.

6 Prompt Strategies — Ranked by Creative Output Tested over 2 weeks
01
Literal
Describe exactly what you want to see. Functions as a production spec.
"Woman, 35, morning light, white bathroom, holding serum bottle"
Low originality
02
Emotional
Describe the feeling, not the subject. Works best layered over other strategies.
"Quiet confidence after a long flight. The stillness before something begins."
Medium
03
Reference-led
Name aesthetics or eras to draw from. Grounds output but risks the derivative.
"Aesop meets early Bottega Veneta. Clean, earned, unhurried."
Low – medium
04
Anti-reference
Define what it must not be. Forces the model into unclaimed territory.
"Not minimal, not maximalist, not Aesop, not Glossier, not clinical."
High
05
Metaphorical
Use analogies from other disciplines to encode the brand's feeling.
"If this brand were an architect, she'd be Zaha Hadid in her early years — ambition before recognition."
Highest
06
Directorial
Write as if on set directing a photographer. Light, angle, moment.
"Late afternoon. She's just finished, not about to start. Light tired but honest. Camera at chest height."
Highest

Literal — Describe exactly what you want to see. Works as a spec sheet, rarely works as inspiration.

Emotional — Describe how the image should feel, not what it should contain. "Quiet confidence after a long flight."

Reference-led — Name aesthetics or eras you want to draw from. Grounds the output but risks the derivative.

Anti-reference — Describe what it should not look like. "Not minimal, not maximalist, not Aesop, not Glossier." This forces the model into a gap that doesn't yet have a name.

Metaphorical — Use analogies from other disciplines. "If this brand were an architect, she'd be Zaha Hadid in her early years — ambition before recognition."

Directorial — Write as if directing a photographer on set. Light quality, camera angle, what the subject is about to do, what just happened.

The directorial and metaphorical approaches consistently produced the most unexpected and ownable results. Literal prompts gave me exactly what I asked for, which was rarely interesting.

The anti-reference strategy was the biggest surprise. Defining the negative space — what the brand refuses to be — created a genuinely unoccupied territory. The model had to fill a gap that existing references couldn't cover.

Emotional prompts worked best as a layer on top of another strategy, not on their own. "Quiet confidence" alone gives you beige. "Quiet confidence + directorial + anti-reference" gives you something you haven't seen before.

The most uncomfortable part of this experiment wasn't the outputs — it was recognising that the skill required to write an excellent prompt is almost identical to the skill required to write an excellent brief. And most briefs are not excellent.

A weak brief produces weak work whether the executor is a human or a model. What AI has done is make the quality of the brief visible immediately, with no human grace to smooth over the gaps.

Build a personal prompt library organised by emotional register — not by subject matter, but by feeling. The best prompts function like a vocabulary of sensation. The better your vocabulary, the more precise your direction.

I'd also spend more time on the anti-reference strategy. Most brand territories are overcrowded. The most useful creative direction is often a description of the negative space no one has claimed.
